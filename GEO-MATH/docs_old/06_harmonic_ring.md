# Harmonic Ring

## Purpose

The previous document established the projective harmonic operators

[
H(\phi)=\cos^2(\phi),
\qquad
H_\perp(\phi)=\sin^2(\phi).
]

The present document studies their interaction.

It will be shown that the harmonic ring is not an additional geometric object but the natural consequence of conservative harmonic complementarity.

---

# Harmonic Pair

The projective operators satisfy

[
H(\phi)+H_\perp(\phi)=1.
]

They represent complementary participation of the same primitive organization.

Their sum measures total participation.

Their interaction measures coupling.

---

# Coupling Definition

Define the active coupling

[
\boxed{
G(\phi)=H(\phi)H_\perp(\phi).
}
]

Using the harmonic representation,

[
G(\phi)
=======

\cos^2(\phi)\sin^2(\phi).
]

Applying

[
\sin(2\phi)=2\sin(\phi)\cos(\phi),
]

gives

[
\boxed{
G(\phi)
=======

\frac14\sin^2(2\phi).
}
]

The ring is therefore generated directly by the interaction of the complementary operators.

---

# Ring Equilibrium

The coupling is maximal when

[
\sin^2(2\phi)=1.
]

Hence

[
2\phi=\frac{\pi}{2},
]

and therefore

[
\boxed{
\phi=\frac{\pi}{4}.
}
]

At equilibrium,

[
H=\frac12,
\qquad
H_\perp=\frac12,
]

and

[
G=\frac14.
]

The observable and complementary sectors contribute equally.

---

# Tangential Dynamics

Differentiating

[
H+H_\perp=1
]

gives

[
H'+H_\perp'=0.
]

Therefore every admissible displacement satisfies

[
\delta H=-\delta H_\perp.
]

The evolution remains tangent to the conservative manifold.

Residual motion is therefore tangential rather than radial.

This explains why hidden structure is redistributed without violating conservation.
