# GEO-ALGEBRA

# Admissible Transformations

## Purpose

The purpose of GEO-ALGEBRA is to characterize the transformations that preserve primitive organization.

Not every mapping between geometric states is acceptable.

Only those transformations that preserve the possibility of conservative reconstruction belong to the GEO framework.

Admissibility therefore precedes computation.

---

# Primitive Concept

Let

\[
T : G \rightarrow G
\]

be a transformation acting on primitive geometry.

The central algebraic question is not whether \(T\) changes representation.

The central question is whether \(T\) preserves primitive identity.

Only transformations satisfying this requirement are admissible.

---

# Definition

A transformation

\[
T : G \rightarrow G
\]

is GEO-admissible if and only if it preserves reconstructability of primitive organization.

Equivalently,

every transformed state remains compatible with at least one representative of the original conservative structure.

Reconstructability is the defining property.

All other admissibility conditions follow from it.

---

# Reconstruction Criterion

For every primitive state

\[
x \in G,
\]

there exists an admissible representative

\[
r
\]

such that

\[
r \sim T(x),
\]

where

\[
\sim
\]

denotes projective equivalence.

Thus transformation preserves equivalence classes rather than particular representatives.

---

# Conservation Theorem

If reconstruction is preserved,

primitive information cannot be destroyed.

Otherwise reconstruction would be impossible.

Therefore every admissible transformation is conservative.

Information may change representation.

It may not cease to exist.

---

# Complementarity Theorem

Since reconstruction requires preservation of the primitive organization,

the complementary sector must remain available.

Therefore admissible transformations preserve complementarity.

Observable and hidden organization remain jointly defined.

Neither sector may be eliminated independently.

---

# Quotient Compatibility

Observable geometry is represented by equivalence classes.

Therefore admissible transformations must satisfy

\[
x \sim y
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
T(x)\sim T(y).
\]

Equivalent representatives remain equivalent after transformation.

The quotient structure is preserved.

---

# Composition Theorem

Let

\[
T_1,T_2
\]

be admissible.

Since both preserve reconstructability,

their composition also preserves reconstructability.

Therefore

\[
T_2\circ T_1
\]

is admissible.

The family of admissible transformations is closed under composition.

---

# Identity

The identity transformation

\[
I:G\rightarrow G
\]

preserves every representative and every equivalence class.

Therefore

\[
I
\]

is admissible.

Identity constitutes the neutral element of the admissible algebra.

---

# Stability

Repeated application of an admissible transformation never destroys primitive organization.

If

\[
T
\]

is admissible,

then

\[
T^n
\]

is admissible for every positive integer

\[
n.
\]

Thus admissibility is stable under iteration.

---

# Observable Interpretation

Observation itself is an admissible transformation.

It changes representation while preserving primitive organization.

Observation therefore belongs to the same algebraic family as coupling, projection and reconstruction.

It is not a fundamentally different process.

---

# Structural Consequences

From admissibility follow immediately:

- conservative decomposition;
- projective equivalence;
- quotient representation;
- representative reconstruction;
- invariant preservation.

These are not independent assumptions.

They are consequences of the admissibility criterion.

---

# Minimality Principle

Among all transformations preserving primitive organization,

admissible transformations introduce no unnecessary structure.

They modify representation only as much as required to preserve reconstruction.

Thus admissibility is the minimal algebra compatible with conservative geometry.

---

# Closing Principle

The defining property of an admissible transformation is not invertibility.

It is reconstructability.

A transformation belongs to GEO if and only if it preserves the possibility of recovering primitive organization through conservative equivalence.

Conservation, complementarity and quotient structure emerge as necessary consequences of this single principle.
