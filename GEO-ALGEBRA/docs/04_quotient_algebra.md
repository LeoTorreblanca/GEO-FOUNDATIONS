# Quotient Algebra

## Purpose

The previous document established that observable identity is determined by projective equivalence.

Primitive representatives differing only by kernel elements are observationally indistinguishable.

The present document proves that observable algebra is therefore naturally defined on quotient classes rather than on primitive states.

---

# Primitive Domain

Let

$$G$$

be the primitive conservative domain.

Primitive representatives contain both observable and complementary organization.

An observer never accesses the full primitive state directly.

Observation acts only through equivalence classes.

---

# Observable Equivalence

By the Projective Equivalence Theorem,

$$x \sim y \iff x - y \in \ker(T)$$

Therefore every observable state corresponds to an entire family of primitive representatives.

The observable object is the class itself.

---

# Quotient Construction

Define

$$[x] = x + \ker(T)$$

The collection of all such classes forms

$$\boxed{G / \ker(T)}$$

This quotient space identifies exactly those representatives that cannot be distinguished observationally.

---

# Well-Defined Operations

Let

$$[x], [y] \in G / \ker(T)$$

Define

$$[x] + [y] = [x + y]$$

Suppose

$$x' \sim x$$

and 

$$y' \sim y$$

Then

$$x' - x \in \ker(T)$$

and 

$$y' - y \in \ker(T)$$

Hence

$$(x' + y') - (x + y) \in \ker(T)$$

Therefore

$$[x' + y'] = [x + y]$$

Addition is independent of representatives.

The quotient algebra is well defined.

---

# Scalar Compatibility

For every scalar

$$\lambda$$

define

$$\lambda[x] = [\lambda x]$$

If

$$x' \sim x$$

then

$$x' - x \in \ker(T)$$

Consequently,

$$\lambda(x' - x) \in \ker(T)$$

Thus

$$[\lambda x'] = [\lambda x]$$

Scalar multiplication is also well defined.

---

# Observable Closure

The quotient is closed under admissible operations.

Every operation performed on representatives descends uniquely to the quotient classes.

Observable algebra therefore possesses its own internal consistency independent of representative choice.

---

# Representative Freedom

Different primitive representatives may describe exactly the same observable state.

Changing representatives modifies hidden organization.

It does not modify observable reality.

Observable physics depends only on quotient classes.

---

# Canonical Projection

Define the canonical projection

$$\pi : G \rightarrow G / \ker(T)$$

by

$$\pi(x) = [x]$$

Every observable quantity factors through

$$\pi$$

Primitive information inaccessible to projection remains encoded in the kernel.

---

# Reconstruction

The projection

$$\pi$$

is surjective but generally not injective.

Consequently,

its inverse does not exist globally.

Reconstruction requires selecting a representative from the quotient together with the complementary kernel organization.

Projection alone is insufficient.

---

# Minimal Observable Algebra

Suppose an observable algebra smaller than

$$G / \ker(T)$$

exists.

Then distinct observable classes would collapse.

Suppose a larger observable algebra exists.

Then kernel representatives would become distinguishable.

Both possibilities contradict projective equivalence.

Therefore

$$G / \ker(T)$$

is the unique minimal observable algebra.
